§2

Research

Proof search asks a deceptively simple question: given a claim, is there a derivation of it — and if so, how do we find one? The theory is usually built on sequent calculi, from Gentzen's original system to modern G3 systems and focusing, alongside automated theorem-proving methods like resolution and tableaux. My thesis asks what happens when the same questions are asked directly in natural deduction — the format closest to how mathematicians actually write proofs — by tracking derivations that are still under construction.

The tension

Proof-search procedures are usually judged on completeness and efficiency. But a derivation in progress doesn't look like a finished proof — it looks like a proof with holes in it. Most formal accounts either idealise this away or fall back on sequent calculus. This thesis keeps the holes, and gives them a name: Partial Proof Terms (PPTs).

Case studies

LJT NJT LKT NKT IC

Five proof systems, spanning intuitionistic and classical logic, reworked to accommodate partial derivations.

Contributions

  • PPT Partial Proof Terms as a new formal tool for studying proof search, applied across five case-study systems.
  • Ext Extension of total proof systems to accommodate partial sequents, with search procedures defined as rewrite rules acting on PPTs.
  • ≅ An isomorphism between focusing and intercalation, connecting two previously separate approaches to proof search.
  • IC An updated Intercalation Calculus (Sieg), integrated with bidirectional natural deduction and focused fragments such as NJT∂.

Future work

  • NJT∂ — extension to classical logic, and a correspondence with a sequent-calculus system.
  • Generalising the focusing/intercalation equivalence to further subsystems and fragments.
  • Integrating proof search with cut-elimination and normalisation on partial derivations.
  • Applying Partial Proof Terms to "less idealised" models of proof search.
  • Generating human-readable output for use in interactive theorem proving.
